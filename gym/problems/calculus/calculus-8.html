Find $\int \frac{x + 1}{(x-1)^2(x-2)}\,dx $.
The factors in the denominator are linear so can integrate by partial fractions.
Let $I = \frac{x + 1}{(x-1)^2(x-2)}$, \begin{align} I & = \frac{a}{(x-1)^2} + \frac{b}{x-1} + \frac{c}{x-2} \\ & = \frac{a(x-2) + b(x-1)(x-2) + c(x-1)^2}{(x-1)^2(x-2)} \end{align} Equating the numerators on both sides, \begin{align} x + 1 & = a(x-2) + b(x-1)(x-2) + c(x-1)^2 \\ & = ax - 2a + bx^2 - 3bx + 2b + cx^2 - 2cx + c \\ & = (b + c)x^2 + (a - 3b - 2c)x + (-2a + 2b + c) \end{align} we then see, \begin{cases} b + c & = 0 \\ a - 3b - 2c & = 1 \\ -2a + 2b + c & = 1 \end{cases} or equivalently, \begin{align} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 & \big| & 0 \\ 1 & -3 & -2 & \big| & 1 \\ -2 & 2 & 1 & \big| & 1 \end{pmatrix} \longrightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 & \big| & 0 \\ 1 & -3 & -2 & \big| & 1 \\ 0 & -4 & -3 & \big| & 3 \end{pmatrix} \\ \\ \longrightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 & \big| & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 & \big| & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \big| & 3 \end{pmatrix} \longrightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & \big| & -2 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & \big| & -3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \big| & 3 \end{pmatrix} \end{align} Hence, $a = -2, b = -3, c = 3$, and so \begin{align} \int I\,dx & = \int \frac{-2}{(x-1)^2}\,dx + \int\frac{-3}{(x-1)}\,dx + \int\frac{3}{(x-2)}\,dx \\ & = \frac{2}{x-1} -3\ln{(x-1)} + 3\ln{(x-2)} \end{align}

Tiffin Girls School

Admission policy

Tiffin Girls is known to be one of the top grammar schools in England. As the only grammar school available to W and SW London families, it attracts a large number of applications, with a near 10:1 applicant/place ratio. On average, 10-15% gain offers from Oxbridge and another 10-15% from Imperial/LSE. Many girls choose to read STEM and medicine subjects.

Unlike private schools, admission is solely based on entrance test scores, without interviews and consideration of extracurricular achievements. There are two test stages, only successful stage one sitters can proceed to stage two. Both stages consist of English and maths. After stage two, a combined mark is calculated at a weight of 60% English and 40% maths. For English, only stage two test score is used, but for maths, it is a weighted average of both stages. Places are then allocated based on this combined mark.

In terms of catchment, the school defines an inner area and a designated area, which includes the former. I prefer to call them inner catchment, outer catchment (designated area minus inner area), and total catchment (their union). The allocation process favours inner catchment candidates, by reserving 60 places for those scoring higher than the 350th mark in the stage 2 candidate pool; those inner catchment candidates not gaining a place during this round will join the remaining pool. The remaining places are then allocated in the order of combined marks in the pool. For inner catchment candidates, they only have to compete against each other for the 60 priority places in a smaller pool, before remaining places become available to the larger pool.

The inner catchment comprises 13 electorial ward areas surrounding Kingston. It is a large area ranging from Richmond riverside to Surbiton, bounded by River Thames and highway A3. The outer area is very wide, covering many areas in Surrey, South West, and West London. They include Epsom, Hampton, Teddington, Richmond, Barnes, Putney, Wandsworth, Wimbledon, Chiswick, and Ealing. The area is defined by postcodes: KT1-10, 12, 13, 17, and 19, TW1-5 and 7-17, SW13-15 and 17-20, W3-5, 7, and 13. Distance to the school only comes into play in the event of a tied-score, so the admission process doesn't otherwise discriminate a candidate from Teddington or Ealing.
Tiffin's inner catchment

Admission data

180 places are usually made available for entry every year. Applications have been steadily growing to 1500 and should materially rise in the coming years due to rising cost of private education. Historically, about 20% of applications are from inner catchment. Only 1/3 move on to stage 2. The inner catchment proportions remain at 20% at stage 2, suggesting lack of performance differentials between inner and outer catchment sitters. While inner catchment sitters make up 20% of the pool, 1/3 of available places are reserved for them; also, those not making it in the priority round can still compete for the remaining 2/3 places.

Inner catchment advantage

Inner catchment applicants have a clear advantage, but about how much. For simplicity, I assume all candidates are equally able so their marks are randomly distributed along a real number line to avoid tied-marks. From past data, I use 1300 and 200, and 370 and 100 for outer and inner catchment sitters in stage 1 and stage 2, respectively.

If there were no catchment priority, we would have all 470 stage 2 sitters compete for 180 places equally. Thus, we expect $\frac{180}{470} \cdot 100 \approx 38$ girls from inner catchment getting admitted. With catchment priority, we can expect more than $60$ girls from the area getting admitted, almost $60\%$ above expectation, signalling a strong bias.

We expect $\frac{350}{470} \cdot 100 \approx 75 $ of inner catchment sitters to score above the 350th mark, so we can expect all 60 places to be allocated during the priority round. Along the equally-spaced 470 line, we expect the lowest rank to gain a priority place to be $\frac{60}{100} \cdot 470 = 282$. On the other hand, we expect the highest-ranked outer catchment sitter to not gain a place to be around $\frac{120}{370} \cdot 470 = 152$. Again, a substantial bias towards inner catchment.

The 40 inner catchment sitters not gaining a priority place are not competitive in the remaining pool; they comprise 25 expected to score below the 350th mark and 15 who are ranked lowest in the group that make the 350th cutoff. Those 15 are expected to be below the $1 - \frac{60}{75} = 20$ percentile in the cohort, so scoring below the $0.8 \cdot 350 = 280$th mark. With only 120 remaining places and 370 outer catchment candidates, admission is not expected for those below such mark, so competition can be seen only between outer catchment candidates. As a result, admission is essentially segregated into a priority round where $33\%$ is allocated to a $20\%$ cohort and another round where $66\%$ is given to a $80\%$ group. On this basis, we expect $\frac{60}{100} = 60\%$ and $\frac{120}{370} = 32\%$ admission rates for inner and outer catchment sitters.

Using the assumptions at stage 2 tests, we have: \begin{align} P(\text{admission} \mid \text{inner catchment}) = {} & P(\text{above 350th mark}) \cdot P(\text{get a priority place}) \\ & {} + P(\text{above 350th mark}) \cdot P(\text{not get a priority place}) \cdot P(\text{get a place}) \\ & {} + P(\text{below 350th mark}) \cdot P(\text{get a place}) \\ = {} & \frac{350}{470} \cdot \frac{60}{75} + \frac{350}{470} \cdot \frac{15}{75} \cdot 0 + \frac{120}{470} \cdot 0 \\ \approx {} & 0.6 \end{align} \begin{align} P(\text{admission} \mid \text{outer catchment}) = {} & \frac{\text{expected places}}{\text{expected candidates in the pool}} \\ = {} & \frac{120}{470-60\text{(priority spots)}-40\text{(uncompetitive inner sitters)}} \\ = {} & \frac{120}{370} \approx 0.3 \end{align} These calculations suggest that the admission advantage for inner catchment is near 2x whatever metrics considered.

  • The (Mis)Behavior of Markets
  • Benoit Mandelbrot
  • This book provides a nice overview of modelling the markets. It does not tell you HOW to model but it does describe some traits of the markets that are not compensated for under the conventional methods, such as Black-Scholes. The author provides a self-contained description of market dynamics and "modern" finance, then goes on to argue that the fundamental assumptions under "modern" financial models are incorrect. The markets do not behave randomly, continuously, or according to normal distribution; investors do not act rationally and their irrational actions can cause further irrational actions through market dynamics. It is interesting to note that Fama, the founder of the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), was Mandelbrot's student; while the former strongly purports market efficiency, the latter refutes it in all vains. Mandelbrot himself developed Fractals and it is such a strong establishment finding applications in various fields. His discussions on volatility clusters, multifractal trading time, and market turbulence are indeed entertaining. The author made a convincing case against "modern" finance and provided some interesting points to be researched. The book is a good read for all levels. However, the reader must note that while Mandelbrot's fractal geometric model might indeed better describe the markets, it is generally not in a better position to predict it and be used as a trading tool (yet).

  • A Mathematician Plays the Stock Market
  • John Paulos
  • This is a similar item as the Random Walk by Malkiel with a more informal tone and vivid examples attached. Even for those who have read the Random Walk, this would still be a good read. Some points, such as market randomness, are overlapping, but others, such as chaos, are new. The author, a math professor, did indeed invest in the stock market himself and almost lost his shirt on WorldCom. It is interesting to find that the author as a math professor fell into some typical psychological traps, such as averaging down and wishful thinking, as an everyday's investor. He emphasizes that asset prices are a result of a series of anticipation of anticipation and that many random events come into play that conventional investment valuations do not work consistently. He discusses the financial market generally and sometimes introduces basic math and stories to desmonstrate the ideas. I personally found chapter 9 From Paradox to Complexity most entertaining in which the self-referential nature of markets is discussed.
